Local foods

Surabaya's culinary delights are a vibrant fusion of rich flavors and local traditions,
offering a taste of Indonesia's diverse and dynamic food culture.

Rujak Cingur

Rujak cingur holds a special place in Surabaya's culinary scene, representing the city's rich blend of flavors and traditions. Rujak cingur is a distinctive version of Indonesia's beloved rujak, traditionally a fruit salad made with various tropical fruits and a spicy-sweet dressing. What sets rujak cingur apart is its unique combination of fruits, vegetables, and an uncommon ingredientâ€”animal muzzle, usually from beef or buffalo. This East Javanese dish blends raw or boiled fruits and vegetables, giving it a diverse texture and flavor profile.

The highlight of rujak cingur is its rich dressing, made with shrimp paste, peanuts, sugar, and chili, which adds a savory and spicy kick to the mix. Typically served on banana leaves and garnished with prawn crackers, the dish often includes rice cakes, tofu, or tempeh as side dishes. Rujak Cingur is a must-try for those seeking a taste of the area's unique culinary heritage.

Pecel Semanggi

Pecel Semanggi is a traditional dish from Surabaya, East Java, made from steamed clover leaves (semanggi) served with a spicy peanut sauce. The dish is often enjoyed with bean sprouts and rice crackers known as kerupuk puli, which are made from rice. Its distinct flavor comes from the sauce, made with sweet potatoes, adding a unique twist to the typical Indonesian pecel. Pecel Semanggi is typically served on banana leaves, adding to its rustic appeal. Originating from the village of Kendung in West Surabaya, this dish reflects the resourcefulness of local villagers who used plants from their surroundings to create this beloved culinary specialty.

Rawon

Rawon is a traditional Indonesian beef soup that hails from the Javanese cuisine of East Java, particularly from the city of Surabaya. This unique dish is known for its distinctive dark color, which comes from the key ingredient, buah kluwek or black keluak nut. The soup is made by slow-braising beef in a rich broth flavored with traditional Indonesian spices like lemongrass, lime leaves, ginger, and chili. The fermentation of the black nut adds an earthy, slightly sour flavor to the dish, creating a complex and savory taste that sets it apart from other Indonesian soups. Today, it is widely served in traditional Indonesian restaurants and is often accompanied by plain rice, salted eggs, bean sprouts, and sambal, a spicy Indonesian chili paste.

Tahu Campur

Tahu campur is a traditional dish from Surabaya, East Java, renowned for its rich flavors. This hearty soup typically features tender beef or cow's trotters (kikil), fried tofu, spiced cassava fritters (lentho), fresh bean sprouts, watercress, yellow noodles, and a shrimp paste-based sauce (petis), creating a savory and umami-packed experience. Garnished with fried shallots and crunchy prawn or garlic crackers, this dish is a popular comfort food sold by street vendors in Surabaya. While originating from the Lamongan area, tahu campur has become a staple in Surabaya's culinary scene, showcasing a delightful blend of savory, spicy, and fresh ingredients that reflects the region's culinary heritage.
